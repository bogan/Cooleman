Weeding

Weeding is the core activity that the group performs on the ridge and is the foundation of the groups strategy to regenerate native vegetation on Cooleman Ridge.

Weeds are a major problem on the ridge. They are a problem in their own right but also a major reason that prevents the ridge form regenerating naturally.

Several different weeding techniques are employed on the ridge, either by the group itself or by one of its partners. This is because no single technique is perfect, with each technique having its own advantages and disadvantages. For instance, some techniques are more effective against some weeds while other techniques are more effective at cetrain times of the year than others. Therefore a suite of techniques are used on Cooleman Ridge to best manage the presence of weeds.

Weeding, however, is not only effective, but is suprisingly enjoyable. Large numbers of weeds can be removed quickly when performed correctly and can lead to both short term and long term imporovements in the native vegetation cover on Cooleman Ridge. An hour or two spent weeding can be a very satisfying experience!

The following is an overview of the specific techniques that are used remove weeds, how they use them and where when they are used.

Hacking

Hacking is the main form of weeding performed the group. It is generally performed with a small pick and simply involves digging out small weeds.

Hacking is most effective against small to medium herbaceous weeds that have a distinctive rosette, such as Aaron's Rod, Wild Sage, Scotch Thistle and Patterson's Curse. Large areas can be clear quickly with minimal effort. It is also a very precise method that removes the weed while leaving natives and is great way to regenerate native grasslands.


Poisoning

Poisoning involves the application of some herbicide to the plant. The herbicide is generally a glyophasate based one that does not harm the enviroment nor humans. Some other more specialised poisons are sometimes used for specific weeds.

Poisons are typically applied using two different methods - spraying or cut-and-dabbing. Spraying is most effective against weeds that have a large rosette such as Pattersons Curse whereas cut-and-dab is best used with woody weeds such as Sweet Briar.

Spraying of specific weeds such as Blackberry is performed by Parks and Conservation officers (or their contractors) using commercial level equipment.


Beheading

Beheading involves the removal of the head or seeding part of the plant while leaving the rest of the plant in place. The head is removed either with some cutting implement or simply by hand.

Although this does not kill the plant, it prevents it from reseeding and thus prevents any future generations from replacing the plant when that plant eventually dies. A concerted course of this technique over several seasons will reduce or eliminate the weed.

This technique is best used with slower growing, annual, tall weeds such as Aaron's Rod and Fleabane.


Burning

Burning can be effective against some weeds that are not very fire tolerant. A regular burn of infested errors with gradually reduce these weeds over time.

Burning not only kills many weeds but more importatly triggers the native plants to germinate. See our Fire page for more details.

Burning is not directly used by the group but is performed by the ACT Rural Fire Service as part of their hazard reduction burn programme. Although the primary intention is not to reduce weeds, burning has many secondary benefits for weed control because it not only kills many weeds but more importatly triggers the native plants to germinate. See our Fire page for more details.


Munching

Munching involves using biological agents such as insects to simply eat the weeds. The muchning may kill the plant or may simply weaken it. Alternatively, it may prevent it from reproducing.

Biological controls exist for some weeds such as St Johns Wort and Pattersons Curse.


Smothering

Smothering or mulching involves covering weeds with some substance such as newspaper, plastic sheets or even the remains of other weeds. Smothering is limited by the availability of materials to smother with.

The blocking of the light starves the weed of light and it can not photosynthesise and eventually dies of starvation.

Smothering is best used on thick infestations of weeds that are difficult to control with other weeds. such as Phalaris.
